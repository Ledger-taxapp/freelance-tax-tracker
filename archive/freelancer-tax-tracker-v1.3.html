Tax terms, explained

New to being your own boss? Here's what these terms actually mean — plain and simple.

Self-employment tax

When you work for an employer, they automatically cover half of your Social Security and Medicare taxes before you ever see your paycheck. When you work for yourself, there's no employer to split that with — so self-employment tax simply combines both halves into one tax you pay directly. It isn't an extra penalty for being your own boss; it's the same tax everyone pays, just landing entirely on your side.

QBI deduction (Qualified Business Income)

This is one of the best parts of being self-employed, and a lot of people don't realize it exists. It lets you deduct 20% of your net business profit before your federal income tax is calculated — meaning roughly a fifth of what you earned from your work isn't taxed at all. You don't need to do anything special to claim it; this tool applies it automatically.

Additional Medicare tax

This only kicks in once income climbs fairly high — above $200,000 for most filers. It adds an extra 0.9% on earnings above that line, on top of the regular Medicare tax. If you're just getting started, there's a good chance this will show as $0 for you, and that's completely normal.

Safe harbor

The IRS wants your taxes paid gradually throughout the year, not all at once in April. Safe harbor is the rule that protects you from a penalty as long as you pay at least a certain amount along the way — usually the lower of either 100% of what you owed last year, or 90% of what you'll owe this year. If you're not sure yet, it's completely fine to leave this blank and come back to it later.

Quarterly estimated payments

Freelance income doesn't have tax withheld automatically the way a paycheck does, so the IRS asks self-employed people to send in payments four times a year instead of one lump sum. This tool spreads whatever you still owe across your remaining due dates for the year, so each payment feels more manageable than one big number in April.

Standard deduction

This is a flat amount the IRS lets everyone subtract from their taxable income before calculating tax — no receipts or paperwork needed. It changes based on your filing status and is already built into every number this tool shows you.

Net vs. gross income

Gross income is everything you brought in before any expenses. Net income is what's left after subtracting your business expenses — and it's the number that actually gets taxed. Two freelancers earning the same gross income can owe very different amounts, simply because one tracked more of their deductible expenses. It's a good reason to log even the small ones.

Standard mileage rate

Instead of tracking the exact cost of gas, maintenance, and wear on your vehicle, the IRS lets you use one simple rate per business mile driven. Multiply your miles by that rate, and that's your deduction. The rate can change mid-year — it did in 2026 — so this tool automatically applies whichever rate matches the date you drove.

W-2 wages

If you also have a regular job alongside your freelance work, this matters more than it might seem. Social Security tax is only collected up to a yearly earnings cap, and wages from your day job count toward that cap first. Telling this tool about your W-2 wages means it won't overcharge you on the Social Security portion of your self-employment tax.
