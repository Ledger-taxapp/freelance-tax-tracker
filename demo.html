Glossary

New to being your own boss? Or a seasoned pro double-checking a detail? Either way, here's what these terms actually mean — plain and simple.

Self-employment tax

When you work for an employer, they automatically cover half of your Social Security and Medicare taxes before you ever see your paycheck. When you work for yourself, there's no employer to split that with — so self-employment tax simply combines both halves into one tax you pay directly. It isn't an extra penalty for being your own boss; it's the same tax everyone pays, just landing entirely on your side.

QBI deduction (Qualified Business Income)

This is one of the best parts of being self-employed, and a lot of people don't realize it exists. It lets you deduct 20% of your net business profit before your federal income tax is calculated — meaning roughly a fifth of what you earned from your work isn't taxed at all. You don't need to do anything special to claim it; this tool applies it automatically. It does start to shrink at higher income — for 2026, it tapers off between $201,775–$276,775 of taxable income (single or head of household) or $403,550–$553,550 (married filing jointly), and this tool follows that same taper.

Federal income tax

This is the tax on your income after your deductions are subtracted, calculated using tax brackets — different slices of your income are taxed at different rates as you earn more, not your whole income at one flat rate. So earning more never means your entire income suddenly gets taxed higher; it just means the next dollar you earn is taxed at a slightly higher rate than the one before it.

Additional Medicare tax

This only kicks in once income climbs fairly high — above $200,000 for single filers and heads of household, or $250,000 for married couples filing jointly. It adds an extra 0.9% on earnings above that line, on top of the regular Medicare tax. If you're just getting started, there's a good chance this will show as $0 for you, and that's completely normal.

Safe harbor

The IRS wants your taxes paid gradually throughout the year, not all at once in April. Safe harbor is the rule that protects you from a penalty as long as you pay at least a certain amount along the way — usually the lower of either 100% of what you owed last year, or 90% of what you'll owe this year. If you're not sure yet, it's completely fine to leave this blank and come back to it later.

Quarterly estimated payments

Freelance income doesn't have tax withheld automatically the way a paycheck does, so the IRS asks self-employed people to send in payments four times a year instead of one lump sum. This tool spreads whatever you still owe across your remaining due dates for the year, so each payment feels more manageable than one big number in April.

Standard deduction

This is a flat amount the IRS lets everyone subtract from their taxable income before calculating tax — no receipts or paperwork needed. It changes based on your filing status and is already built into every number this tool shows you.

Net vs. gross income

Gross income is everything you brought in before any expenses. Net income is what's left after subtracting your business expenses — and it's the number that actually gets taxed. Two freelancers earning the same gross income can owe very different amounts, simply because one tracked more of their deductible expenses. It's a good reason to log even the small ones.

Standard mileage rate

Instead of tracking the exact cost of gas, maintenance, and wear on your vehicle, the IRS lets you use one simple rate per business mile driven. Multiply your miles by that rate, and that's your deduction. The rate can change mid-year — it did in 2026 — so this tool automatically applies whichever rate matches the date you drove.

W-2 wages

If you also have a W-2 job alongside your freelance work, this matters more than it might seem. Social Security tax is only collected up to a yearly earnings cap, and it's based on your own earnings record — not your household's. Telling this tool about your own W-2 wages means it won't overcharge you on the Social Security portion of your self-employment tax.

Spouse's W-2 wages

This one only shows up for joint filers, and it plays a different role than your own W-2 wages. Your spouse's earnings don't affect your Social Security cap — that's tracked per person — but they do count toward your household's combined income for federal tax brackets and the Additional Medicare Tax. Keeping the two separate is what lets this tool get both calculations right at once.

W-2 withholding

Every paycheck from a W-2 job already has federal tax withheld and sent to the IRS automatically — the IRS treats that money as if it were paid evenly across the whole year, no matter when it was actually withheld. This tool counts that withholding toward what you owe, alongside any estimated payments you've logged, so it doesn't ask you to pay twice on income your employer is already covering. You can usually find a running total on your pay stub, or estimate it from your most recent one.

Already covered

This adds together two different things: any W-2 withholding you've told this tool about, plus any estimated payments you've logged in the ledger. Together, they're what's already chipping away at your tax bill — the "Suggested next payment" is just what's left after subtracting this from your target.

Effective tax rate

This is your total projected tax divided by your net business income — in other words, roughly what share of what you actually kept ends up going to taxes. It's usually lower than the tax bracket you're in, since brackets only apply to the top slice of your income, not all of it.
